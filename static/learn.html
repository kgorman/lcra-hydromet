HOW THE HIGHLAND LAKES WORK

Six dams, two jobs, ninety years of compromise.

The Highland Lakes look like a vacation postcard. Underneath, they're an industrial system — a chain of reservoirs built between 1935 and 1951 to do two things the Colorado River had never done willingly: stop drowning Austin during floods, and keep delivering water through Texas droughts. The trade-offs in that mission shape every gate operation you see on the live dashboard.

THE BIG PICTURE

Two storage lakes do almost all the work.

Of the six Highland Lakes, only Lake Buchanan and Lake Travis actually store significant water. The other four — Inks, LBJ, Marble Falls, and Austin — are pass-through reservoirs. They stay within roughly a foot of their normal elevation year-round because they're too small to matter for storage; their job is to make electricity, support recreation, and ferry water from Buchanan down to Austin.

When you see Lake Travis drop 60 feet in a drought, that's the basin's water supply being spent. When Lake LBJ stays glued to 825 feet through the same drought, it's not because LBJ is "doing better" — it's because LBJ is engineered to stay there.

THE CHAIN

Upstream to downstream.

The six dams sit along a 150-mile stretch of the Colorado River from near Burnet down through downtown Austin. Water released from one lake flows directly into the next.

Buchanan and Travis hold 99%+ of the system's usable water. Inks is essentially a re-regulation pool between Buchanan and Wirtz Dam. Lake Austin sits at the foot of the chain, just above Lady Bird Lake (held by Longhorn Dam, owned by the City of Austin — not part of the LCRA chain).

FLOOD CONTROL

The Army Corps owns the top of Lake Travis.

Mansfield Dam is the keystone of flood operations on the lower Colorado. Lake Travis has a conservation pool up to elevation 681 ft (the level you usually see in boating photos) and a flood pool from 681 ft to 715 ft — the next 34 vertical feet of capacity reserved for catching floodwaters. Everything above 681 ft belongs, by statute, to the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers.

When a major storm system enters the basin, USACE's Fort Worth District takes the decision-making lead on Mansfield's floodgates under a federal Water Control Manual. LCRA physically operates the gates and runs the math, but the orders to open or hold are made in coordination with the Corps. This is why the dashboard shows the floodgate status of Mansfield Dam — and the inflows into Lake Travis — as the single most consequential row.

Buchanan also has floodgates, but its flood pool is shallower (~5 ft) and its job is largely to keep its own footprint contained while passing inflows through to Travis. Inks, Wirtz, and Starcke are fixed-crest dams — they have no floodgates. When water comes in, water goes out, and the lake stays at its normal level.

WATER SUPPLY

The Water Management Plan rations a finite resource.

Lake Travis and Lake Buchanan supply drinking water to more than a million people, cool power plants, irrigate rice in the lower basin, and keep the river flowing for the downstream environment all the way to Matagorda Bay. LCRA does this under a Water Management Plan approved by the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ).

The plan distinguishes between two kinds of customers:

  • Firm water — cities, power plants, industry. Always served, even in extreme drought, down to the worst drought of record.
  • Interruptible water — primarily rice agriculture downstream. Curtailed or cut off when combined storage drops below preset triggers.

This is why the combined storage of Buchanan + Travis is the headline number for drought conditions. When you hear "the lakes are at 38% full" on the news, that's the combined-storage figure, not a single lake.

HYDROELECTRIC + RECREATION

Every dam is also a power plant.

All six dams have hydroelectric units. The pass-through lakes (Inks, LBJ, Marble Falls, Austin) generate continuously to take advantage of the steady flow through them. Buchanan and Mansfield generate opportunistically — when water is being released downstream for supply or flood reasons anyway, the turbines spin.

LCRA was founded in 1934 explicitly to bring electricity to rural central Texas. The Highland Lakes were the New Deal infrastructure that delivered on that mission. The recreational economy that grew up around the lakes — boating, lakehouses, tourism — is downstream of (no pun intended) the engineering decisions made in the 1930s and 40s.

TIMELINE

How we got here.

  • 1934
    LCRA chartered by the Texas Legislature.
    A state-created public utility tasked with controlling floods, conserving water, and bringing electricity to a 10-county Hill Country region. Funded initially through New Deal Public Works Administration grants.
  • 1937
    Buchanan Dam construction completed (impoundment 1939).
    Originally started in 1931 by a private power company that went bankrupt; LCRA finished it. The largest reservoir in the chain. Named for U.S. Rep. James P. Buchanan, who championed the project.
  • 1938
    Inks Dam goes into service.
    The smallest of the chain — a re-regulation pool just below Buchanan that smooths out the variable releases from Buchanan's turbines.
  • 1940
    Tom Miller Dam at Lake Austin rebuilt.
    The site had been dammed since 1893; two prior structures had failed in floods. The 1940 concrete arch dam (still in service) closed the southern end of the chain. Owned by the City of Austin, operated by LCRA.
  • 1942
    Mansfield Dam completes Lake Travis — the flood control workhorse.
    Jointly funded by LCRA and the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. The federal investment bought the top 34 feet of flood-pool capacity, permanently establishing USACE's role in flood operations on the lower Colorado.
  • 1951
    Wirtz Dam (Lake LBJ) and Starcke Dam (Marble Falls) close the chain.
    The two final pass-through reservoirs. Lake LBJ was originally named Granite Shoals; renamed in 1965 for President Lyndon B. Johnson, whose family ranch sat on its shoreline.
  • 1980s–present
    Hydromet network grows to 275+ automated gauges.
    The data feeding this dashboard. Telemetry from gauges all over the basin reports every 10–15 minutes, used by LCRA, USACE, the National Weather Service, and downstream emergency managers.
  • 2008–2016
    Drought of record stress-tests the Water Management Plan.
    Combined Buchanan + Travis storage dropped below 33% of capacity, triggering interruptible water curtailment. The drought drove a comprehensive update to the WMP — the framework still in use today.
WHEN THINGS GET INTERESTING

What to watch on the dashboard.

  • Lake Travis head elevation creeping above 681 ft means USACE is starting to make decisions about flood-pool storage versus releases.
  • Mansfield Dam gateOps changing from "no gate operations expected" to anything else is the single most important signal on the page.
  • Inflows into Lake Travis spiking — visible upstream as elevated stage/flow on the river gauges (San Saba, Llano, Pedernales) — precede gate decisions by hours.
  • 24-hour rainfall bubbles clustering high in the Buchanan basin upstream means storage gains are coming. High in the Travis basin closer to Austin means runoff is going to hit Mansfield fast.
